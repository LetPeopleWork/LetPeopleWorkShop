# Improv Prototyping

- **Purpose:** Turn abstract fixes for a recurring problem into *enacted* behaviors — small groups invent and act out solutions as improv skits, so the answer becomes a memorable, repeatable interaction instead of a slide bullet.
- **When to use:** A chronic interpersonal / process problem keeps recurring (a bad handoff, a tense customer moment, a meeting that always derails) and talking about it hasn't changed it. Use when you need people to *practice* a new behavior, not just agree to it.
- **Group config:** Whole group names the recurring problem -> small groups (4-6) each develop a skit -> perform to all -> all debrief and harvest the moves that worked.
- **Timing:** ~45 min (5 min frame the problem, 15 min invent + rehearse skits, 15 min perform, 10 min harvest). Trim to ~30 min with fewer groups performing.
- **Medium fit:**
  - In-person: ideal — bodies, space, props, and an audience. The physical enactment is the point.
  - Video: workable but weaker — groups improvise in breakouts and perform on camera; energy and movement drop. Keep skits very short and lean on the debrief.
  - Miro: poor fit — this is performed live, not boarded. Omit.
- **Steps:**
  1. Name a recurring, frustrating situation the group faces repeatedly. Make it concrete (a real moment, real roles).
  2. Small groups invent a short skit that prototypes a *better* way to handle it — and rehearse it.
  3. Each group performs its skit live (60-90 sec). The audience watches for moves that work.
  4. Harvest: "What specific behaviors did we see that we want to keep?" Capture the repeatable moves, not vague principles.
- **Facilitator notes:** The trap is letting it become "describe your solution" instead of *acting it out* — the value is in the embodiment, so insist groups perform, not narrate. Also resist over-rehearsal; rough improv surfaces more honest behavior than a polished script. The harvest is where abstract value lives: pin down the exact gesture or sentence that worked so people can reuse it Monday morning.
