# Helping Heuristics

- **Purpose:** Practice giving and receiving help *without* fixing, imposing, or rescuing — through role-play rounds that reveal how often "helping" is actually controlling, and what genuinely useful help feels like.
- **When to use:** Teams where help defaults to advice-giving and problem-takeover; coaching / mentoring skill-building; any group that needs to collaborate as peers rather than experts-and-novices.
- **Group config:** Triads (client / coach / observer), rotating roles across rounds; if numbers force it, pairs work for the simpler rounds.
- **Timing:** ~45 min (brief the three helping styles, then 3 practice rounds of ~8-10 min each with role rotation, then debrief). Trim to ~30 min with shorter rounds or pairs.
- **Medium fit:**
  - In-person: excellent — quiet triads around the room, easy role rotation, rich body-language cues.
  - Video: works well in 3-person breakouts; assign roles in the room name and rotate each round. Cleaner than most role-plays online because it's all conversation.
  - Miro: not a board activity — the practice is spoken. Omit.
- **Steps:**
  1. Introduce three modes of helping: directing (telling), following (pure service), and partnering (co-creating). Name how each feels.
  2. In triads, one is the client with a real issue, one the coach/helper, one the observer.
  3. Round 1 — coach practices one style; observe what happens. Debrief in the triad.
  4. Rotate roles and repeat across the helping styles so everyone plays each part.
  5. Whole-group debrief: "What did good help feel like to receive? What pulled us toward fixing?"
- **Facilitator notes:** The point lives in the *practice rounds plus debrief*, not the model — don't over-lecture the three styles, get people role-playing fast. The recurring revelation is how strong the pull to "fix" is, especially for senior/expert people; name that out loud. Protect the observer role: their feedback on what they saw the helper *do* is often sharper than the client's own felt sense.
