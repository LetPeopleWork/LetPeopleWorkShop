# Remember the Future

- **Source detail:** Luke Hohmann, *Innovation Games*. ("Innovation Games" is a registered trademark of Luke Hohmann — technique attributed here; use no branded materials.) Mechanic rests on *prospective hindsight* from cognitive psychology.
- **Purpose:** Surface concrete, specific goals by inviting people to stand in a future where success has *already happened* and "remember" what the product/change *did* to make them successful.
- **When to use:** Kicking off an initiative, defining what "good" looks like, or turning a fuzzy aspiration into specific requirements — when "what do you want?" produces vague wishlists.
- **Group config:** Solo write -> small-group share -> cluster as whole group.
- **Timing:** ~30-45 min (frame the future ~5, solo write ~10, share + cluster ~20). Trim to ~20 with one quick share round.
- **Medium fit:**
  - In-person: a drawn timeline to a future date; people post "memories" as stickies. Excellent.
  - Miro: a future-dated frame; cards written in past tense, then clustered.
  - Video: shared doc with the future date as a header; breakouts to write, plenary to cluster.
- **Steps:**
  1. Pick a future date far enough out that success could plausibly have happened (e.g. "12 months from today").
  2. Frame it as already past — note the tense: "It's [date]. This has been a success. Looking back, **what did it do** to make you successful?"
  3. Individuals write what happened — concrete outcomes phrased as *memories*, not wishes.
  4. Share, cluster; the recurring "what it did" items become the real goals / requirements.
- **Facilitator notes:** The grammar IS the trick — "what **did** it do" (prospective hindsight) pulls far more specific and complete answers than "what should it do." Enforce past tense ruthlessly; the instant people slip into "it should..." you're back to an ordinary wishlist. Works well beyond product — any change effort. The negative mirror is `pre-mortem` (imagine it *failed*); running both gives you goals and risks in one sitting.
