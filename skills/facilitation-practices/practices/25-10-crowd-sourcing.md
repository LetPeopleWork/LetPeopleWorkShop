# 25/10 Crowd Sourcing

- **Purpose:** Rapidly generate and sift a crowd's boldest ideas, letting the strongest rise via anonymous peer scoring rather than the loudest advocate or the most senior sponsor.
- **When to use:** You want a fast, energetic shortlist of bold actions from a large group, and you want courage rewarded — ideas that people would hesitate to champion out loud.
- **Group config:** Whole group milling and exchanging cards; scoring done individually; top results read out to All.
- **Timing:** ~30 min (5 min write, ~5 rounds of pass-and-score at ~2-3 min each, 5 min reveal). Trim to ~20 min with fewer scoring rounds; expand by adding a round or a follow-on action step.
- **Medium fit:**
  - In-person: index cards passed hand-to-hand while milling. Excellent — the physical shuffle is the anonymity engine.
  - Miro: anonymous card duplication + a quick star/number vote across the board. Workable, but you lose the energizing churn; randomize card order so authorship stays hidden.
  - Video: poor fit — there's no clean way to pass cards anonymously and keep the milling energy. Drop it.
- **Steps:**
  1. Pose the invitation: "If you were ten times bolder, what big idea would you recommend? What's your first step?" Each person writes idea + first step on one index card.
  2. On "go," everyone mills and passes cards around continuously until told to stop — nobody should know whose card they hold.
  3. Each person reads the card they're holding and scores it 1 (low) to 5 (high), writing the number on the back. Pass again.
  4. Repeat scoring for 5 rounds, so each card collects 5 independent scores. Each holder sums the five numbers.
  5. Ask for the cards scoring 25, then 24, then 23... reading out the top ideas until you have your shortlist.
- **Facilitator notes:** The anonymity is the whole point — scorers must not know the author, which is why the milling and continuous passing matter; if people score their friends' cards, bold-but-uncomfortable ideas get buried. Keep the pace brisk and a little chaotic. Needs a real crowd (15+) so scores are diverse; with a small group authorship leaks and the magic dies.
