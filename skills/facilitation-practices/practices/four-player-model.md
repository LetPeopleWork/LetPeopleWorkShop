# The Four-Player Model (conversational roles)

- **Source detail:** David Kantor's **structural dynamics**; operationalized for facilitation by Marsha Acker, *The Art & Science of Facilitation*. A lens for reading *how* a group is talking, beneath *what* it's saying. Attribute the model to Kantor.
- **Purpose:** Diagnose in real time *why a conversation is stuck* by noticing which of four speech-acts are present, missing, or jammed — then deliberately invite the missing one.
- **When to use:** A sanity-check lens across **any discussion**; most load-bearing when a group keeps circling, agrees suspiciously fast, or one person dominates. Run it continuously, never as an agenda row.
- **Medium fit:** all — it's about who's doing what act, not where.

## The four actions (anyone can take any, any moment)
1. **Move** — sets direction, proposes ("let's do X").
2. **Follow** — supports, completes, builds on a move ("yes, and we could...").
3. **Oppose** — challenges, questions, corrects the move — the guard against groupthink.
4. **Bystand** — observes and names the pattern from the margin ("I notice we keep returning to budget").

These are **actions, not personalities** — the same person moves, then follows, then opposes.

## How to read the room
- **All Moves, no Follows** → ideas pile up, nothing lands. Invite a Follow.
- **No Opposes** → false harmony / groupthink; "agreement" that isn't real. Invite an Oppose.
- **Opposes heard as personal attacks** → people stop opposing → fake yeses. Legitimize dissent.
- **No Bystanders** → the group can't see its own pattern. Offer a Bystand yourself.

## Facilitator notes
The single most useful move is **legitimizing the Opposer** — in most teams opposition is socially punished, so it goes underground and resurfaces as quiet non-commitment (the exact mechanism behind a counterfeit yes). When you hear suspicious agreement, actively invite an oppose and *thank* it. Watch for the senior person monopolizing Move; explicitly ask others to Move. The full structural-dynamics theory is much larger — this Four-Player slice is the usable, citable core.
