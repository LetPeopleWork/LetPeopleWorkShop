# TBR Brain Rules — the Six Trumps

- **Source detail:** Sharon Bowman, *Training from the BACK of the Room*, grounded in brain science /
  accelerated learning. A lens for designing sessions the brain actually engages with.
- **Purpose:** maximize attention, retention, and participation by designing with how brains learn —
  not against it.
- **When to use:** a sanity-check lens across **any** design; most load-bearing for content-/learning-heavy
  sessions. Liberating Structures already embody most of these, so use the rules to spot *gaps*.
- **Medium fit:** all — it's about how brains engage, not where.

## The Six Trumps (each beats its traditional opposite)
1. **Movement trumps sitting** — build in physical movement (stand, move to the wall, switch partners/seats).
2. **Talking trumps listening** — learners talk about the content; facilitator talks <30% of the time.
3. **Images trump words** — use visuals, metaphors, drawing; make thinking visible on walls/boards.
4. **Writing trumps reading** — have people write (in their own words) *before* they discuss.
5. **Shorter trumps longer** — chunk into ~10–20 min segments; no long lecture blocks (the "10-minute rule").
6. **Different trumps same** — vary the activity, your voice, the modality, the grouping for novelty.

## Plus
- **WIIFM (What's In It For Me)** — state relevance up front so people opt in (belongs in *Connections*).
- **Primacy–Recency** — people remember **openings and closings** best → open and close strong, and prefer
  *more, shorter* segments (more beginnings/endings = more memorable moments).

## Facilitator notes
These are **heuristics, not a checklist to robotically tick**. LS structures (1-2-4-All, TRIZ, …) already
maximize talking/writing/movement/variety, so an LS-heavy design is mostly brain-friendly by default.
Use the rules to catch the lurking problems: a long lecture block, a session with no movement, a weak or
rushed close, or relevance never made explicit (no WIIFM).
