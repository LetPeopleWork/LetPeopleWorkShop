# What I Need From You (WINFY)

- **Purpose:** Surface unspoken cross-functional interdependencies by having each functional group state its top needs FROM each other group, with every request answered by one of just five crisp responses — no speeches, no defensiveness.
- **When to use:** Several functions/teams that depend on each other but talk past each other, where needs go unstated and resentment builds. Best when 3-7 distinct functional groups are present.
- **Group config:** People split into functional groups -> each group agrees its top needs from each other group -> structured request-and-response round across all groups.
- **Timing:** ~70 min (form groups + clarify the 5 responses 10, groups draft their needs 20, request round 30, debrief 10). Trim to ~55 min with fewer functions and tighter drafting.
- **Medium fit:**
  - In-person: groups at separate tables; a spokesperson stands to deliver each need to a named group, who answers with one of the five words. The ritual formality is what disciplines it.
  - Video: each function in a breakout to draft, then a tightly-facilitated plenary for the request round; the facilitator must enforce the one-word-response rule hard, which is harder remotely.
- **Steps:**
  1. Split into functional groups (by team/role/function).
  2. Teach the FIVE allowed responses and nothing else: "Yes", "No", "I'll try", "Whatever" (meaning your request is irrelevant to me), and "I need more information".
  3. Each group agrees its top 2-3 needs from each other group, phrased as a specific request: "What we need from you is...".
  4. Request round: one group at a time states a need to a specific other group. The receiving group gives ONE of the five responses — no explanation, no negotiation, no speeches.
  5. Record every request and its response. After all rounds, debrief what the pattern of responses revealed.
- **Facilitator notes:** The entire power is in the no-speeches rule — the instant you let a group explain or defend a response, it collapses into the usual circular meeting. Hold the five-word discipline ruthlessly, even when it feels rude; the discomfort is the data. "I'll try" and "Whatever" are the most revealing answers — note where they cluster. Save all problem-solving for AFTER the round; mixing it in destroys the clarity.
