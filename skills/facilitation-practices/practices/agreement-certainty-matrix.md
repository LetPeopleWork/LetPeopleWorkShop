# Agreement-Certainty Matrix

- **Purpose:** Sort a pile of challenges by how much agreement and how much certainty surrounds each, so the group stops applying tidy fixes to messy problems and picks an approach that fits the actual zone.
- **When to use:** A group faces a mix of issues and keeps reaching for plans/best-practices on things that are genuinely complex or chaotic; you want shared language for "this one isn't solvable by a checklist."
- **Group config:** Whole group or small groups plot challenges on a shared 2x2, then discuss and sort to All.
- **Timing:** ~30 min (5 min explain the matrix, 10 min plot challenges, 10 min discuss zones + approaches, harvest). Trim to ~20 min with a pre-collected list of challenges; expand to ~40 min to choose a method per zone.
- **Medium fit:**
  - In-person: a large 2x2 drawn on the wall, challenges on stickies placed by the group. Excellent.
  - Miro: a 2x2 frame with draggable cards. Strong — easy to re-sort as the discussion shifts.
  - Video: shared 2x2 slide/board, participants place items by annotation or call-out. Works well.
- **Steps:**
  1. Draw the matrix: vertical axis = degree of **certainty** (close to / far from certainty), horizontal axis = degree of **agreement** (close to / far from agreement).
  2. Name the zones (after Stacey): high agreement + high certainty = **Simple**; far on one axis = **Complicated**; far on both = **Complex**; very far on both = **Chaotic**.
  3. Have the group list the challenges they're facing, one per sticky/card.
  4. Plot each challenge by asking: how much do we agree on what to do? how certain are we of the outcome? Place it in the matrix.
  5. Discuss what each zone implies: Simple -> apply best practice; Complicated -> expert analysis/good practice; Complex -> probe-sense-respond, run safe experiments; Chaotic -> act to stabilize first. Choose fitting approaches.
- **Facilitator notes:** The payoff is the conversation about WHERE a challenge sits, not the neat grid — expect disagreement on placement and treat that as data (low agreement IS the diagnosis). The classic trap is plotting everything as Simple/Complicated because those feel controllable; gently test "are we really certain of the outcome?" to push items into Complex where they belong. Pair Complex-zone items with experiment-based structures rather than detailed plans.
