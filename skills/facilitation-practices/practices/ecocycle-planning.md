# Ecocycle Planning

- **Purpose:** Map all of a group's activities onto the infinity-loop ecocycle (birth -> maturity -> creative destruction -> renewal) to see the whole portfolio at once — what to start, what to sustain, and crucially what to let go.
- **When to use:** When the to-do list is overloaded, everything feels equally urgent, and the group needs to see that some activities are stuck and others should be deliberately ended. Great for portfolio reviews and "we keep adding but never subtracting."
- **Group config:** Individuals list activities -> place each on a shared ecocycle map -> whole group reads the portfolio -> 1-2-4-All on the two traps -> agree actions.
- **Timing:** ~95 min full (list activities 15, place on the map 25, read the portfolio 15, work the traps 25, decide actions 15). Trim to ~35 min for a small activity list and a single-group placement.
- **Medium fit:**
  - In-person: draw the big infinity loop on a wall; each activity is a sticky placed along the curve. Seeing them cluster is the insight.
  - Miro: an ecocycle template image as the background; drag activity cards onto the loop.
  - Video: shared whiteboard/Miro with the loop; facilitator or participants place cards while talking through them.
- **Steps:**
  1. Each person lists the activities/programs/commitments they're involved in (one per sticky/card).
  2. Place each activity somewhere on the ecocycle loop: birth (new, fragile), growth, maturity (productive, established), creative destruction (declining, should end), renewal (reorganizing, recombining).
  3. Read the whole portfolio together. Where is everything bunched up?
  4. Hunt the two traps: the RIGIDITY TRAP (stuck in over-mature, can't let go of what's past its prime) and the POVERTY TRAP (good new ideas that never get resources to be born).
  5. Decide: what do we let go of (free up resources), what do we protect to renew, what do we launch? Assign owners.
- **Facilitator notes:** The "creative destruction" arc is the one people avoid — they'll cheerfully place things in birth and maturity and leave nothing in the let-go zone. Push for honest placement; the entire value of the structure is permission to STOP doing things. The rigidity-trap conversation (step 4) is usually the most uncomfortable and the most valuable; don't rush past it to the fun of launching new things.
