# Shift & Share

- **Purpose:** Spread innovations, projects, or new ideas across a group *without* a marathon of plenary presentations — presenters staff stations and give a short demo or story to small rotating groups, so everyone sees several up close and intimately.
- **When to use:** Many parallel projects/teams to share; when big formal presentations would bore everyone and only reach passive listeners. Good for surfacing what's working, cross-pollinating, and starting conversations.
- **Group config:** Several stations (one presenter each) + small groups that rotate from station to station.
- **Timing:** ~25-70 min depending on station count (each round ~5-7 min including a question or two, plus rotation time). Trim by running fewer stations or shorter rounds; expand with more stations and a closing harvest.
- **Medium fit:**
  - In-person: excellent — physical stations around the room, small groups walk from one to the next. Movement keeps energy up.
  - Miro: workable — stations as board frames with the presenter's demo, groups "rotate" frame to frame (paired with breakout rooms per station). The spatial layout maps cleanly.
  - Video: poor for *pure* video — rotating small groups across presenters is clunky and the movement/intimacy is lost. Only attempt with disciplined breakout-room rotation; otherwise omit.
- **Steps:**
  1. Set up stations — one per project/innovation, each with a presenter ready to give a short demo or story (not a slide deck).
  2. Divide participants into small groups, one per station to start.
  3. Each presenter gives their short pitch; the small group asks a question or two.
  4. Ring a bell — groups shift to the next station. Presenters stay and repeat.
  5. Continue until groups have seen the stations that matter; close with a quick harvest of what spread.
- **Facilitator notes:** The whole point is to kill the boring all-hands slide deck — coach presenters to *demo or tell a story* in a few minutes, repeatedly, to small groups, not to lecture. Keep groups small (3-5) so the station feels like a conversation, not an audience. Time the rotations tightly and signal clearly; presenters repeating their bit several times is by design, and it's how the ideas actually land. Reassure presenters that repetition gets sharper each round.
