# Panarchy

- **Purpose:** Make visible how a change has to move across NESTED system levels — individual, team, org, sector — that each cycle at different speeds, so the group can see where to push and why change stalls between levels.
- **When to use:** Advanced systems work — when a change keeps stalling and you suspect it's because levels above or below are working on a different clock. For groups grappling with adoption that won't scale up or down.
- **Group config:** Whole group names the nested levels -> small groups map activity/cycles at each level -> whole group traces cross-scale influences (remember + revolt) -> identify leverage.
- **Timing:** ~90 min (orient to the model 15, name the levels 10, map each level in groups 25, trace cross-scale connections 25, find leverage 15). Trim to ~60 min with fewer levels and a tighter cross-scale trace.
- **Medium fit:**
  - In-person: stack the levels vertically on a wall (individual at bottom -> sector at top), each as its own ecocycle loop; draw arrows between levels.
  - Miro: nested/stacked ecocycle loops as a template; draw cross-scale arrows ("remember" downward, "revolt" upward) between them.
  - Video: shared board with the stacked levels; works but the abstraction is harder to hold remotely — keep the visual central at all times.
- **Steps:**
  1. Orient the group to the panarchy idea: smaller, faster cycles (individual) nested inside larger, slower ones (sector), each on its own ecocycle.
  2. Name the relevant nested levels for your situation (e.g. me -> my team -> the org -> the wider field).
  3. For each level, map where it sits in its own cycle — what's growing, mature, being destroyed, renewing.
  4. Trace the two cross-scale moves: "revolt" (a fast small-level collapse triggering change upward) and "remember" (a slow large level providing stability/memory downward).
  5. Identify leverage: where can a change at one level unlock movement at another? Where is one level holding the others stuck?
- **Facilitator notes:** This is the most abstract Liberating Structure — do not run it with a group that hasn't already done Ecocycle Planning, or you'll spend all your time explaining the model instead of using it. The real payoff is the "remember/revolt" trace in step 4: it explains why a great team-level change dies (the slow org level "remembers" the old way and pulls it back), and where a small revolt can actually tip a larger level. Keep the visual stacked and central; the insight is spatial.
