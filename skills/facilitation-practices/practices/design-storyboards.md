# Design StoryBoards

- **Purpose:** Make a plan concrete and shareable by storyboarding it step by step — small groups lay out the sequence of a session, product, or process as a visible series of frames, so it can be inspected and tested *before* anyone commits.
- **When to use:** Designing a meeting, workshop, rollout, or process and you want it to read as a sequence of real moves rather than a vague intention; when a plan needs to be handed off or pressure-tested by others.
- **Group config:** Small groups (4-6) each build a storyboard; basic = a single linear sequence, advanced = layered detail (per-step purpose, who, timing, materials).
- **Timing:** Basic ~25 min; advanced up to ~70 min (frame the design task 5 min, build the storyboard 15-45 min, share + critique 10-20 min). Trim by staying basic and skipping the layered detail.
- **Medium fit:**
  - In-person: excellent — sticky notes or index cards in a row on a wall, easy to re-order and annotate.
  - Miro: strong fit — frames or a card row left-to-right; reordering and adding detail layers is trivial on the board, and the result is instantly shareable.
  - Video: weak without a shared canvas — storyboarding is inherently spatial. Use Miro for remote. Omit.
- **Steps:**
  1. Frame the thing being designed and its goal.
  2. Small groups storyboard it as an ordered sequence of frames — each frame one step/moment.
  3. (Advanced) Add layers to each frame: purpose, who's involved, timing, materials, what could go wrong.
  4. Groups post storyboards and walk others through them.
  5. Critique and refine: where does the sequence break, what's missing, what to test first?
- **Facilitator notes:** Insist on *sequence* — the value is seeing the flow step by step, not a bullet list of components; if a group writes a list, push them to put it in order and show transitions. Keep frames movable (stickies / cards) so re-sequencing is cheap — that re-ordering is where design flaws get caught. For advanced, don't add all detail layers at once; get the spine of the sequence right first, then layer.
