## Book 1 — Gamestorming (Gray, Brown, Macanufo; O'Reilly, 2010)

### Orientation
Gamestorming positions itself as "a set of co-creation tools used by innovators around the world" — a toolkit for "innovators, rule-breakers and changemakers" [S1]. Its core contribution is twofold: (1) a **meta-structure for any creative working session** — the *opening / exploring / closing* arc, driven toward a deliberately under-specified "fuzzy goal" — and (2) a **large catalog of named visual games** (Empathy Map, Cover Story, 3-12-3, Pre-mortem, etc.) organized by intent (fresh thinking, design, problem-solving, decision-making, vision/strategy) [S1]. The book is widely credited with popularizing visual/game-based facilitation in product and design teams. Note: many games it catalogs are *aggregated* from other authors (e.g. Empathy Map from XPLANE/Osterwalder; Speedboat from Hohmann's Innovation Games), so attribution must be checked per-game.

### Signature methods (mechanics traced to source)
- **The Opening/Exploring/Closing arc + "fuzzy goal"** — a meta-framework: every session diverges (opening), explores/experiments (exploring), then converges (closing); the session aims at a "fuzzy goal" that is clear in direction but intentionally vague in specifics, leaving room for emergence [S1]. _Cross-ref pending second source._
- **Empathy Map** — a stakeholder-understanding canvas around a stylized head, divided into Thinking/Feeling, Seeing, Hearing, Saying & Doing (and often Pains/Gains). Project into the stakeholder's perspective and fill each zone with sensory detail; ~20 min for a rough map. Created by **XPLANE**, popularized by Osterwalder's *Business Model Generation* — NOT original to Gamestorming [S2]. (Attribution caveat.)
- **Cover Story** — a visioning game: groups (4-6 per template) imagine future success already reported on a magazine cover (main story, headlines, quotes, sidebars, images), written in past tense; 5 min solo + 30-45 min group + 5-10 min present; ~90 min max. Surfaces collective aspiration; shared themes are the harvest [S3].
- **3-12-3 Brainstorm** — a tightly timeboxed ideation: **3 min** individual generation of observations on cards (no filtering) -> **12 min** pairs draw 3 random cards and build a rough concept -> **3 min** each pair presents (3-min cap). ~21-30 min total, up to ~10 people. Speed prevents overthinking; works standalone or as an energizer [S4].
- **Pre-mortem** — prospective risk surfacing: at kickoff, with goals/plan set, ask "imagine this failed — what went wrong?"; team names risks from experience, then ranks/votes and assigns mitigations. ~5 min/participant; small teams. Source attribution in Gamestorming is "unknown"; the *technique* is academically attributed to **Gary Klein** (prospective hindsight); Gamestorming itself notes similarity to Hohmann's "Remember the Future" [S5]. (Attribution caveat.)
- **Forced Ranking** — a decision tool: stakeholders rank a finite set of options against explicit criteria into a strict order (no ties), forcing trade-off clarity. _Cross-ref pending; flagged single-source until confirmed._
- **Other catalog games** named in the brief (Affinity Map, Dot Voting, Post-Up, Heart-Hand-Mind, Speedboat): Affinity Map / Post-Up / Dot Voting are generic clustering & voting primitives; Speedboat is Hohmann's (see Book 2).

### Overlap vs. gap analysis
- **Empathy Map** — GAP. No empathy/stakeholder-perspective canvas in our LS+TBR set. The closest, `simple-ethnography`, is field observation, not a projected-persona canvas. Strong, widely-cited, distinct.
- **Cover Story** — PARTIAL OVERLAP with `design-storyboards` and `cover`-style visioning, but distinct mechanic (future-press framing). Overlaps in *intent* with LS visioning but no LS does the "media cover" device. Borderline GAP.
- **3-12-3 Brainstorm** — OVERLAP in intent with `1-2-4-all` and `25-10-crowd-sourcing` (fast divergent ideation + surfacing), but the timeboxed 3/12/3 cadence with random-card recombination is distinct. Marginal value given strong existing ideation structures.
- **Pre-mortem** — GAP. We have no prospective-risk / failure-imagination structure. `critical-uncertainties` and `agreement-certainty-matrix` address uncertainty differently (scenario/sense-making), not "imagine we failed." High value, distinct.
- **Forced Ranking** — OVERLAP. Our `25-10-crowd-sourcing` and dot-voting-style prioritization (and `agreement-certainty-matrix`) cover prioritization. Generic; low marginal value.
- **Opening/Exploring/Closing arc** — PARTIAL OVERLAP conceptually with Kaner's Diamond (divergent->convergent). As a *framework* type it could anchor agendas, but Kaner's Diamond (Book 4) is the more rigorously-sourced, widely-taught version of the same divergent->converge macro-arc. Prefer Kaner's.

## Book 2 — Innovation Games (Luke Hohmann; Addison-Wesley, 2006)

### Orientation
*Innovation Games: Creating Breakthrough Products Through Collaborative Play* presents collaborative play as a form of **primary market research** — customers play directed games to surface their *true, hidden needs and desires* that direct questioning fails to elicit [S6][S7]. The book delivers twelve named games, each engineered around a cognitive insight (e.g. prospective hindsight, projective design). It is a cornerstone of product-discovery and agile product-management facilitation. **Trademark caveat:** "Innovation Games®" is a **registered trademark** of Luke Hohmann; the IP later moved through The Innovation Games Company -> Conteneo -> acquired by Scaled Agile (2019) [S8]. Use of the marks/branded materials carries licensing considerations; the *underlying techniques* are widely taught and citable, but we should attribute carefully and avoid implying endorsement. (Also note: the canonical domain innovationgames.com currently redirects to an unrelated/parked site — do NOT cite it as a live source.)

### Signature methods (mechanics cross-referenced across [S6][S7])
- **Speed Boat (Sailboat)** — a boat held back by **anchors**; participants write disliked features/pain points as anchors, depth = severity. Surfaces what's slowing customers down. (This is the method Gamestorming catalogs as "Speedboat.")
- **Remember the Future** — participants imagine a future date after sustained product use and "remember" what the product *did* to make them successful. Exploits prospective hindsight: asking "what did it do" yields richer, more complete answers than "what should it do." Strongly evidence-backed (cognitive psychology) [S7].
- **Prune the Product Tree** — a tree drawn on a wall; limbs = functionality areas, edge = current release; customers add leaf-shaped cards for desired future features, revealing which branches matter for growth. Roadmap-shaping.
- **Buy a Feature** — customers receive limited (often pooled) budget and "buy" desired features priced by cost/effort; collaboration/negotiation forces real prioritization and reveals willingness-to-trade.
- **Product Box** — participants design and "sell" the packaging of their ideal product using craft materials; the marketing pitch surfaces what they truly value.
- **20/20 Vision** — features on cards ranked by importance via pairwise comparison against a reference card on the wall; produces a forced priority order.
- **Spider Web** — maps how the product connects to everything else in the customer's world (central node + relationship lines); reveals integration/context.
- **Start Your Day** — customers document when/how/where they use the product across timeframes (routine and crisis); contextual usage mapping.
- **Give Them a Hot Tub** — a shuffled card set deliberately seeded with absurd/outrageous features; customer reactions distinguish the truly essential from the noise.

### Overlap vs. gap analysis
- **Speed Boat / Sailboat** — GAP (with caveat). No LS/TBR equivalent for "what's holding us back" anchor-visualization. Very widely cited (also in Gamestorming, Agile retrospectives). High value. Attribute to Hohmann; the generic "Sailboat retrospective" form is in the public domain of practice.
- **Remember the Future** — GAP. Distinct, evidence-backed prospective-hindsight technique. The Gamestorming Pre-mortem is the *negative* mirror (imagine failure); Remember the Future is the *positive* (imagine success). Neither exists in our set. High value.
- **Buy a Feature** — GAP. Budget-constrained collaborative prioritization with negotiation is mechanically distinct from dot-voting (`25-10-crowd-sourcing`) — scarcity + trading is the point. High value. Trademark-attribution caveat.
- **Prune the Product Tree** — GAP. Roadmap-shaping visual metaphor; no LS equivalent. Niche (product context) but distinct.
- **Product Box** — PARTIAL OVERLAP with `design-storyboards` / `cover story` (projective design artifacts). Distinct device but overlapping intent.
- **20/20 Vision** — OVERLAP with prioritization tools (`25-10-crowd-sourcing`, Forced Ranking). Generic, low marginal value.
- **Spider Web / Start Your Day** — PARTIAL OVERLAP with `simple-ethnography` / `social-network-webbing` (context & relationship mapping). Lower marginal value.
- **Give Them a Hot Tub** — niche add-on to Buy-a-Feature-style sessions; low standalone value.

## Book 3 — The Art and Science of Facilitation (Marsha Acker; 2020)

### Orientation
*The Art & Science of Facilitation: How to Lead Effective Collaboration with Agile Teams* is the most **practitioner-development-oriented** of the four. Its thesis: facilitation is "an inside-out practice, starting with the facilitator's own belief system, awareness and development" — i.e. the facilitator's **self is the instrument** [S9]. Rather than a catalog of activities, it offers **lenses and stances** the facilitator holds *across* any agenda: the Five Guiding Principles of the **Facilitation Stance**, and a working application of **David Kantor's structural dynamics** (the Four-Player Model and four speech acts) for reading what is happening beneath the words [S9][S10]. Acker is a Certified Professional Facilitator and Certified Structural Dynamics Interventionist (Kantor Institute), so the Kantor material is applied first-hand, not second-hand [S10]. These map to our **`principle`** type (lenses, no timing), not `structure`.

### Signature concepts (cross-referenced [S9][S10])
- **The Facilitation Stance / "self as instrument"** — facilitation begins with the facilitator's own awareness, neutrality, and beliefs; the Five Guiding Principles teach inviting divergent views (even when they create conflict), staying unbiased in high-stakes meetings, sensing what the group needs, and navigating interpersonal dynamics [S9]. A **principle/lens**, applied throughout an agenda.
- **The Four-Player Model (Kantor)** — every functional conversation needs four acts/roles: **Mover** (initiates, proposes direction), **Follower** (supports, completes, builds on), **Opposer** (challenges, corrects course — guards against groupthink), **Bystander** (observes, offers perspective from the margin) [S10]. Stuck conversations show role imbalance (e.g. all movers, no followers; absent opposers). Facilitators diagnose the pattern and invite the missing act. A **principle/lens** [S10]. (Attribution: model is Kantor's; Acker operationalizes it for facilitation.)
- **Structural dynamics / listening for the underlying structure** — reading the *form* of a conversation (who is doing what act, in what domain, in what operating system) rather than just its content, to spot the pattern creating "stuckness" and intervene deliberately [S10]. A **principle/lens**.

### Overlap vs. gap analysis
- **Facilitation Stance / self as instrument** — GAP. Our library is almost entirely activity-structures plus a few TBR teaching lenses; we have **no facilitator-disposition/neutrality principle**. This fills a real gap (the "how to *be* as facilitator" lens). High value as a `principle`.
- **Four-Player Model** — GAP. We have no conversational-role diagnostic. Distinct, well-sourced (Kantor lineage), broadly applicable as a `principle`. High value. Attribute to Kantor via Acker.
- **Structural dynamics (full)** — PARTIAL OVERLAP with the Four-Player principle; the full model is deep and arguably beyond a single practice card. Recommend capturing the *Four-Player lens* as the citable, usable slice and referencing structural dynamics as its source, rather than trying to encode the whole theory.

## Book 4 — The Facilitator's Guide to Participatory Decision-Making (Kaner et al.; Jossey-Bass, 3rd ed. 2014)

### Orientation
Kaner et al.'s *Facilitator's Guide to Participatory Decision-Making* is arguably the single most-cited reference in group-decision facilitation. Its core contribution is a **theory of how groups actually move toward sound, supported decisions** — captured in the **Diamond of Participatory Decision-Making** — together with a set of operating values (full participation, mutual understanding, inclusive solutions, shared responsibility) and concrete tools, most famously the **Gradients of Agreement** [S11][S12][S13]. The Diamond and Groan Zone are the book's signature, near-universally taught contributions.

### Signature concepts (cross-referenced [S11][S12][S13])
- **The Diamond of Participatory Decision-Making** — a macro-arc map of group process: an opening **Divergent Zone** (surfacing diverse perspectives, options, data, dissent), a middle **Groan Zone** (the struggle to integrate competing perspectives — confusion, irritation, repetition; the hard, necessary middle), and a closing **Convergent Zone** (organizing, refining, selecting) leading to **Closure** (decision rule + choice) [S11][S12]. This is a **framework** type — an arc you map structures onto.
- **The Groan Zone** — Kaner's coined term for the predictable, uncomfortable middle phase. The key facilitator insight: do not avoid or rush it; it is where genuine integration happens. Tools: build shared context (paired interviews, deeper purpose), strengthen relationships, normalize the discomfort [S11][S12]. (Closely tied to the Diamond; can be taught as a `principle` lens — "expect and protect the Groan Zone.")
- **Gradients of Agreement** — a decision/consensus-testing tool replacing binary yes/no votes with an **8-point scale**: (1) wholehearted endorsement, (2) endorsement with minor reservation, (3) support with concerns, (4) agreement with major reservations, (5) mild opposition, (6) strong opposition, (7) veto, (8) blocking. Participants signal their position; the facilitator sets the required threshold in advance, then discusses outliers and re-tests. Simplified 4-point variant: "Love it / Like it / Live with it / Loathe it." Makes implicit disagreement visible and prevents false consensus [S13]. A **structure** (a timed, run-able activity) or a decision `principle`. _(Note: the 4-point "Fist of Five"/"Roman vote" family is a folk descendant; Kaner's is the canonical, citable version.)_
- **Core participatory values** — full participation, mutual understanding, inclusive solutions, shared responsibility [S11]. A **principle** lens that frames the whole approach.

### Overlap vs. gap analysis
- **Diamond / Divergent-Groan-Convergent** — GAP. We have no overarching divergence->convergence framework. This is the canonical, best-sourced macro-arc — more rigorous than Gamestorming's opening/exploring/closing (which is a looser cousin). High value as a `framework`. Prefer this over Gamestorming's arc.
- **Groan Zone (as a lens)** — GAP / high value. Distinct facilitator mindset; nothing in LS/TBR names or normalizes the integration struggle. Strong `principle` candidate (can be folded into the Diamond framework or stand alone).
- **Gradients of Agreement** — GAP. Our prioritization tools (`25-10-crowd-sourcing`, dot-voting, `agreement-certainty-matrix`) are about *ranking options*, not *testing support for a proposed decision*. Gradients fills the "are we actually agreed / how strongly" gap. Very widely cited, distinct. High value as a `structure`.
- **Core participatory values** — PARTIAL OVERLAP in spirit with LS's design ethos and Acker's Facilitation Stance. Worth referencing but lower standalone-card value than the three above.

### Source-quality caveats
1. **Trademark — Innovation Games®.** "Innovation Games®" is a **registered trademark** (Hohmann -> The Innovation Games Company -> Conteneo -> Scaled Agile, 2019) [S8]. The *techniques* (Speed Boat, Remember the Future, Buy a Feature) are widely taught and citable, but practice cards should attribute to Hohmann and avoid branded logos/materials or implying endorsement. Speed Boat also circulates as the generic "Sailboat retrospective."
2. **Dead/hijacked canonical domain.** `innovationgames.com` currently **redirects to an unrelated parked/gambling site** — do NOT cite it as live. Use `lukehohmann.com` (author-primary) and Wikipedia/Wind4Change for cross-reference.
3. **Attribution drift in Gamestorming.** Several "Gamestorming" games are aggregated from elsewhere: **Empathy Map** = XPLANE/Osterwalder; **Speedboat** = Hohmann; **Pre-mortem** technique = **Gary Klein** (Gamestorming lists source as "unknown"). Attribute to original authors.
4. **Kantor lineage.** The Four-Player Model is **David Kantor's** (structural dynamics); Acker operationalizes it. Cite Kantor as origin, Acker as the facilitation application.
5. **Single-source / weaker-sourced flags.** Gamestorming's **Forced Ranking** and the **fuzzy goal/arc** detail were confirmed from the Gamestorming site only (one source) at research time — flagged; not proposed for addition anyway. Acker's **Five Guiding Principles** detail came primarily from publisher/promo pages [S9] plus the Kantor cross-ref [S10]; the granular five-principle list would benefit from the book directly before writing a card.
6. **Gradients of Agreement variants.** The 8-point scale is Kaner's canonical version [S13]; the 4-point "Love it/Like it/Live with it/Loathe it" and folk "Fist of Five / Roman vote" are descendants — cite Kaner as the authoritative source.

## Conflicting Information

1. **Pre-mortem attribution.** Gamestorming lists the source as "unknown" and notes similarity to Hohmann's Remember the Future [S5]; the academic/practitioner consensus attributes the *premortem* technique to **Gary Klein** (prospective hindsight, HBR 2007). Resolution: attribute to Klein; note Gamestorming as a popularizing catalog.
2. **Empathy Map origin.** Commonly associated with Gamestorming, but [S2] itself credits **XPLANE** (and popularization by Osterwalder's *Business Model Generation*). Resolution: attribute to XPLANE.
3. **Number of Innovation Games.** The book delivers "twelve" games [S7][S8]; Hohmann's site highlights nine [S6 lists 12 incl. Me and My Shadow, Show and Tell, The Apprentice]. Resolution: the core set is the nine product-discovery games; the observation games (Me and My Shadow, The Apprentice, Show and Tell) round out the twelve. No material conflict for our purposes.
4. **Diamond "zones" count.** Sources variously list 3 zones (divergent/groan/convergent) [S13 summary] or 4-5 phases adding "business as usual" and "closure" [S11][S12]. Resolution: the canonical teaching is the three-zone diamond + closure; document it that way.
